Note 021 / Memes + Culture

Why every crypto community eventually becomes a religion

A field note on rituals, sacred charts and the social utility of believing together.

By @429999group 21 July 2026 4 min read
Side note 01

No community calls its own behavior strange. That job belongs to everyone watching from outside.

Spend enough time inside any crypto community and the language starts to change. Ordinary market updates become prophecies. Founders become reluctant saints. A roadmap stops being a plan and becomes a set of sacred promises that must be interpreted correctly.

I do not mean this as an insult. Religion is one of humanity’s oldest technologies for creating coordination, identity and meaning. Crypto communities are simply running a newer version on faster servers, with more screenshots and worse sleep schedules.

The rituals arrive first

Every community develops a rhythm. There are weekly spaces, daily greetings, repeated slogans and the ceremonial reposting of the same chart. The chart may show users, transactions or token holders. Its exact meaning matters less than the act of gathering around it.

Rituals create continuity. Markets are chaotic, timelines are forgetful and product development is slow. A shared ritual tells people that the group still exists even when nothing important happened that week.

“The token is the membership card, but the shared language is what makes the room feel real.”

Belief needs an enemy

A strong group identity usually needs a boundary. There are believers and tourists, builders and extractors, early users and late liquidity. Sometimes there is a rival chain with suspiciously similar goals and unforgivably different branding.

The boundary is useful because it turns a complicated technical thesis into a social choice. You no longer need to understand every tradeoff. You only need to know which side has your profile picture.

Research note

Changing the profile picture appears to increase technical confidence by an amount not recognized by science.

My favorite part is the folklore

Communities remember near disasters, mysterious wallets, old posts and accidental jokes. These stories become portable. New members learn them and repeat them. What began as a random moment becomes part of the group’s explanation for itself.

This is where memes do serious work. A meme compresses history, emotion and position into something that can survive a fast timeline. It is not the opposite of research. It is a different storage format.

Belief can build, but it can also hide

Shared conviction helps people coordinate through uncertainty. It can finance experiments that look ridiculous from a safe distance. It can also make criticism feel like betrayal and turn every warning into proof that outsiders do not understand.

The useful question is not whether a community behaves like a religion. Most durable groups do. The useful question is whether its beliefs remain open to reality. Can the group update? Can it laugh at itself? Can the sacred chart be wrong?

I trust communities that can keep the ritual and lose the certainty. They are usually more interesting, and much easier to share a group chat with.

Footnotes

  1. This note is cultural observation, not a statement about any specific token.
  2. If your community has no sacred chart, the sacred chart may simply not have been posted yet.
